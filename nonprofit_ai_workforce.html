AI and your staff

Augmentation, capacity, and the conversations to have before adopting
Mandy Hathaway Consulting  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics & Technology Consulting

"Is AI going to replace my grant writer?" The question shows up in some form in almost every conversation about AI in nonprofit settings, and it usually shows up uncomfortably, because nobody wants to be the leader who answered it wrong in either direction. This guide is for executive directors, HR leads, and team supervisors who need a frame for thinking about AI's effect on their staff, and a conversation to have with the team before AI changes the work.

The short answer to the replacement question: probably not in the way the hype suggests. The longer answer involves a more interesting question: what does AI free your staff to do, and what does it quietly take away. Most of the harm in nonprofit AI adoption right now is not job loss. It is what happens to capacity, to morale, and to the kinds of work people are allowed to spend time on.

The risk to your staff is not that AI replaces them. It is that AI raises the expected pace of their work without giving any of that new capacity back as breathing room.

What AI actually does to nonprofit roles

The pattern across early adopters is consistent. AI does not eliminate roles. It changes the mix of tasks within roles. The tasks that go first are the ones people often wish they could offload: first drafts of routine communications, summary documentation, repetitive formatting, basic research synthesis. The tasks that remain are the ones that require relationship, judgment, accountability, and being known to clients and partners.

For most nonprofit jobs, this is genuinely good news. People did not enter the sector to spend hours formatting board packets or drafting acknowledgment letters. They entered to do work that matters to people, animals, or the planet. If AI handles the formatting, more time goes to the work.

That is the promise. The reality is often different, because the time saved tends not to stay saved.

The Impact Treadmill

Compass Pro Bono, working with NTEN, named a pattern that captures the underlying risk: the Impact Treadmill. The mechanism is simple. AI lets a person produce more in the same hours. The organization adjusts expectations upward to match the new capacity. The person produces more, and is no less busy. Repeat.

Over a year, this looks like growth. Over three, it looks like burnout. The capacity gain from AI is real, but it goes to output rather than back to the staff member who created it. The cumulative effect is a workforce running faster and faster on the same caloric intake.

The leadership choice

When AI frees up two hours a week for a staff member, the org has to decide explicitly where those hours go. Do they go to more output? To deeper relationship work that has been underfunded? To rest? To training? The default, if no one decides, is more output. That default is the treadmill.

Where augmentation actually helps

AI works best as augmentation in roles where the constraint is time-on-task rather than judgment. Reviewing all of the following with a specific question in mind ("does AI here give back capacity, or just absorb it") will tell you where to start.

  1. Development directors. Drafting acknowledgments, appeal hooks, donor research synthesis. AI helps with the volume work that was already pulling them away from relationship time.

  2. Communications coordinators. Newsletter drafts, social media variants, press release skeletons. Most of this work was always a translation problem: turn organizational happenings into reader-friendly language. AI is reasonable at that translation.

  3. Grant writers. Standard sections (organizational background, prior outcomes, demographic context), reading-level adjustments, first-pass drafts of new proposals from established templates. The work that remains is the work that matters most: funder strategy, relationship management, and the framing decisions that AI cannot make.

  4. Executive directors. Board materials, internal communications, summarizing long documents before meetings, drafting talking points for tricky conversations. AI handles the volume; the ED still does the thinking.

  5. Operations and admin. Meeting notes, scheduling triage, data summaries, onboarding checklists. Usually high-volume, low-judgment work where AI is straightforwardly useful.

Where AI does not help, or actively makes things worse

  1. Direct service work. Case managers, advocates, counselors, job coaches. The relationship is the work. AI for documentation may help. AI for the actual service interaction is almost always wrong.

  2. Crisis response. Any work that involves people in acute distress should be done by humans. AI prompts, suggested scripts, automated triage all degrade response quality in ways that compound over time.

  3. Personnel and HR work. AI for screening resumes is increasingly common and increasingly problematic. Even when accurate on average, the failures are concentrated in ways that reproduce bias. Use cautiously and with explicit human review at every step.

  4. Eligibility decisions. Whether someone gets housing, benefits, services. Never use AI as the decider. AI as one input to a human-made decision is acceptable only with strong policy guardrails.

  5. Client-facing language at scale. Mass-personalized messages to clients who experience trauma or marginalization read as automated. People can tell. The credibility cost is larger than the time savings.

The conversation to have with your team before adopting

The single biggest mistake in nonprofit AI rollouts is announcing the tool before having the conversation. The conversation worth having is short, and works best in a small staff meeting or in the first round of one-on-ones after the decision is made.

Five questions worth asking the team

1. What parts of your job do you wish you could offload? (This is the AI starting point.)

2. What parts of your job are the reason you are here? (This is what AI should not touch.)

3. If AI gave you two hours a week back, where do you most want those hours to go?

4. What concerns do you have about AI in your role, including ones that feel hard to say out loud?

5. What would have to be true for you to feel safe telling us when AI is and isn't working for you in six months?

The point of the conversation is not consensus. The point is making the choice visible. AI adoption that happens around staff produces shadow AI and resentment. AI adoption that happens with staff produces feedback, iteration, and a workforce that helps you get this right.

The reskilling and capacity question

For most nonprofit roles, "reskilling" is the wrong frame. AI use is more like adopting a new tool than learning a new profession. The skills that matter are: clear thinking about what you want, basic prompt construction, judgment about when AI output is wrong, and discipline about data hygiene. None of these require coding or a certification.

What does require investment: time to learn. Staff who are told to "start using AI" without dedicated learning hours will either ignore the instruction or use AI badly. A reasonable budget is two to four hours of paid learning time per staff member to set up tools, build first assistants, and practice. Then ongoing support: someone on staff who can answer questions, a shared library of prompts that work for your organization, and a quarterly check-in on what is and is not working.

The burnout adjacency

Nonprofit staff are already at high baseline burnout risk. AI can worsen this or improve it, depending on how leadership handles the capacity question above. Three signals to watch for:

Burnout signals worth catching early

Staff producing more output but reporting flat or worsening job satisfaction. Staff using AI but feeling guilty about it. Staff saying they "could not do their job without AI" within a few months of adoption. Each of these is a sign that capacity is being captured, not returned.

A discussion guide for executive directors

Run this in a thirty-minute staff meeting. Print or share the questions in advance so people have time to think.

  1. Open with the org-level commitment. "We are exploring AI tools to make some kinds of work easier. We are committing to: not using AI to replace staff, returning at least some of the time saved as breathing room, and being honest about what is not working."

  2. Ask the five questions above. Take notes. The goal is to listen, not to solve.

  3. Name what AI will not be used for. This is the trust-building step. "AI will not be used for performance reviews. It will not be used for client decisions. It will not be used to draft anything that goes out under a staff person's name without their review."

  4. Identify the first two or three workflows. Pick small. Pick optional. Let staff opt in.

  5. Set a check-in date. Three months out. Same questions. Honest answers expected.

AI adoption in nonprofits is going to happen one way or another. The choice is whether it happens deliberately, with staff voice and capacity protection built in, or by default, with the impact treadmill running. The first version takes more time at the front end and produces better outcomes at the back end. The second version is faster to start and tends to break things slowly.
