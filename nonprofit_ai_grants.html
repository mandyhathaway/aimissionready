AI for grants work

Tools, funders, discount programs, and the ethics of AI in proposals
Mandy Hathaway Consulting  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics & Technology Consulting

Grants work is one of the highest-leverage places to use AI in a nonprofit, and one of the easiest places to use it badly. The same generative tools that can free up hours of drafting time can also produce proposals that sound interchangeable, lose your organization's voice, leak confidential strategy, or break the implicit (and increasingly explicit) trust between you and your funders.

This guide covers the four things grants teams actually need to know: which tools are worth your time, which funders are worth knowing about, which discount programs cut your software costs, and how to think about disclosure to funders when AI was used in drafting.

The fastest way to lose a funder is to send them a proposal that sounds like every other proposal in their inbox. AI can produce that proposal in twenty minutes. The work of avoiding it is still yours.

AI tools for grant writing, ranked by where they actually help

Most "AI grant writing" tools are wrappers around the same underlying models you can use directly. The wrappers can be worth it for the workflow design, or they can be a tax. Sort by what they actually save you, not by what they promise.

Tier 1 — The general-purpose chat tools

Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, all with custom assistants set up for your org (see the Getting More From Any AI guide). The best fit for organizations that already have a development director who can prompt deliberately. Cheapest option per seat. Most flexible. Will not have grant-specific features baked in, but those features mostly turn out to be templates, not magic.

Tier 2 — Purpose-built grant AI

Tools like Cause Writer AI and similar grant-specific platforms. Worth a look if your team writes a high volume of small-to-mid grants and needs the workflow opinionation. Validate data terms carefully: many grant-specific tools were built on top of OpenAI's API with consumer-tier protections.

Tier 3 — Prospect research with AI augmentation

Instrumentl, Candid Foundation Directory, GrantStation. The AI features layer onto existing grants databases and help with prospecting and timing. Sometimes useful, often replicate what good filtering already does. Try the free trials before subscribing.

Tier 4 — Don't bother

Standalone "AI grant proposal generator" tools that promise complete proposals from a few inputs. The output is uniformly mediocre and easily identifiable to anyone who reads more than a few proposals. The time saved is borrowed against your funder's goodwill.

What to use AI for in grants work

  • Drafting first passes of standard sections (organizational background, prior outcomes, demographic data write-up) where you have a strong source-material library to ground the work
  • Rewriting existing language at a different reading level or for a different audience
  • Brainstorming opening hooks, headlines, or framings for LOIs
  • Translating funder language into your organization's terms, and vice versa
  • Drafting budget narratives from spreadsheet data
  • Generating critique questions for your own draft ("identify the three weakest claims in this proposal")
  • Building reusable language libraries for repeated grant elements
  • Compiling reports from program data where the source material is structured and non-confidential

What to never use AI for in grants work

  • Drafting any section that requires specific client outcomes from individual cases without anonymization
  • Generating fake or composite client stories that will be presented as real
  • Submitting AI-generated content to a funder who has explicitly prohibited or asked about AI use, without disclosure
  • Pasting in confidential funder feedback or rejection language from previous cycles into a tool that may train on it
  • Using AI to predict which proposals will win, then over-tailoring future work to those patterns
  • Auto-submitting proposals across multiple funders without human review of each

Discount programs that cut AI tooling costs

Many AI tools have nonprofit-discount tiers that get overlooked because the discount paperwork lives in a different place than the tool itself. Worth checking eligibility for each.

  1. TechSoup. The central clearinghouse for nonprofit software discounts. Most major vendors verify eligibility through TechSoup. Annual fee is modest. Start here if you have not already.

  2. Google for Nonprofits. Free Workspace, ad grants, discounted Gemini access. Eligibility verified through Percent or TechSoup.

  3. Microsoft for Nonprofits. Discounted 365, Azure credits, Copilot access. Bundled discount structure means it often pays for itself even before AI features.

  4. OpenAI for Nonprofits. Discounted ChatGPT Team. Eligibility via Percent. Discount tier varies; verify current rates before assuming.

  5. Canva for Nonprofits. Free Canva Pro for eligible orgs, including the Magic Studio AI features.

  6. Zapier for Nonprofits. 15 percent off most plans. Useful when AI workflow automation enters the picture.

  7. Grammarly for Nonprofits. Business-tier discount. Worth using over the consumer tier for any team deployment.

  8. Loom for Education and Nonprofits. Free or discounted plans, including AI summary features.

For Anthropic (Claude), the discount situation is fluid as of mid-2026. Worth contacting their sales team directly for mission-aligned pricing if Claude is your primary tool.

The ethics of AI in grant proposals

Funders are increasingly asking. Some have started including questions in proposal forms: "Did AI tools contribute to this proposal? If yes, in what way?" Others are working it into due diligence after the submission. The conversation is happening sector-wide, and the standard answers are still settling.

A useful working principle: disclose AI use the way you would disclose any other tool that materially shaped the document. You do not list every spell-check correction. You would mention that the proposal was drafted with consultant assistance, or that the budget was prepared by your finance director. AI assistance falls in roughly the same category for most uses.

Practical disclosure principle

If AI was used to brainstorm, draft, edit, or refine, a sentence in the cover letter or in a footnote naming that use is appropriate. If AI was used to generate the entire proposal with minimal human input, that is a different situation and probably should not have happened. If the funder asks directly, answer directly and specifically.

The funders most likely to ask about AI right now are technology funders, AI-specific funders, and foundations with explicit values around authenticity and voice in proposals. Smaller community foundations are typically less focused on the question, but the conversation is moving in their direction too.

Three habits that prevent AI from making your grant writing worse

  1. Build a grounded source library before you build a prompt. Your case for support, your last three successful proposals, your strategic plan, your evaluation framework, your style guide. Upload these into a Custom GPT or Project. The AI now drafts from your real material rather than generic training data.

  2. Have someone read it out loud before you submit. AI-generated text has a cadence that sounds fine on the page and obvious in the ear. If a sentence makes the reader stumble, rewrite it.

  3. Compare every AI draft to your previous successful proposals on voice, not just content. The fastest way to lose your organizational voice is to gradually let AI re-shape it across grant cycles. If your proposals stop sounding like you, you have a problem regardless of what funders are saying.

AI does not write better grant proposals than experienced humans do. It writes the same kinds of proposals faster, with all the same risks. The development directors who get the most out of these tools are the ones who treat AI as a junior staff member who needs supervision, not as a replacement for the work they have spent years getting good at.
