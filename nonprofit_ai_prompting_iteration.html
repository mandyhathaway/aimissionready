Prompting & iteration

How to refine when the first output is mediocre
Mandy Hathaway Consulting  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics & Technology Consulting

Most AI outputs are mediocre on the first try. The people who get genuinely good results from these tools are not the ones who write better opening prompts. They are the ones who keep going past the first response, refine, push back, and treat the conversation as a draft cycle rather than a vending machine.

This guide covers what to do when the first output is not what you wanted. It pairs with the Prompt Library (which gives you starting templates) and the Getting More From Any AI guide (which covers the setup that makes iteration cheap). Read those for context. Read this for the moves that turn a thirty-percent draft into something worth using.

The first output is the draft. The third output is usually the keeper. The seventh is when you've taught it something it can reuse.

The three loops that fix almost any output

Most "the AI got it wrong" moments fit into one of three patterns. Knowing which pattern you're in tells you which loop to run.

  1. Clarify the ask. The AI is producing the wrong thing because it misunderstood what you wanted. Symptom: the output is well-written, but it answers a question you didn't ask. The fix: tell it specifically what was off, then restate the actual ask.

  2. Give it an example. The AI is producing the right kind of thing but in the wrong form. Symptom: the content is fine, but the tone, length, or structure is off. The fix: show it one or two examples of what you do want, then ask for another in that style.

  3. Ask it to critique itself. The AI is producing something it thinks is good, but the standard is too low. Symptom: the output sounds confident but is full of clichés or generic claims. The fix: ask the AI to identify the three weakest parts of its own output, then rewrite those.

The "make it" verbs that actually work

The fastest way to refine a draft is also the most underused: just tell the AI what to change.

Phrases that produce a meaningful edit

Make it shorter. Make it longer. Make it more direct. Make it warmer. Make it cooler. More concrete. Less abstract. Less marketing-tone. Less defensive. Strip the qualifiers. Cut the hedging. Open with a question instead. Open with a number. Replace the second paragraph entirely. Keep the structure, replace the content. Reorder by importance. Reorder by chronology.

Each of these is a complete instruction. You do not need to explain why. The AI can usually figure out what "warmer" means in context. If the first attempt overshoots, push the other direction by half: "less warm, more peer-to-peer." Iteration converges fast.

A worked example: a donor thank-you that needed five turns

This is a real-ish conversation, lightly edited. The starting prompt was decent, but every turn made the output better.

Turn 1 — original prompt Draft a thank-you note to a long-term donor who just gave a mid-level gift. Three paragraphs, warm but specific. No "generous support" or "transformative." Around 200 words.
Turn 1 — output Dear [Donor], Thank you for your continued partnership with [Organization]. Your gift this year will help us advance our mission of serving the community with the kind of dedication and care that defines our work… [continues for 220 words in the same tone]
Turn 2 — fix This sounds like every donor letter ever written. Make it specific. Open with a moment, not a thank-you. Reference one actual thing the gift will support, not "our mission."
Turn 2 — output Dear [Donor], Tuesday's intake had eleven people waiting at 7 am. Three of them stayed for the full day. The reason that's possible is the after-hours pantry shift your gift funds, which means we don't have to send people home before they've eaten…
Turn 3 — fix Better. Now tighten. Cut anything that sounds like fundraising. I want this to read like a personal note from the director.
Turn 5 — output (after two more passes) Tuesday's intake had eleven people waiting at 7. The pantry shift your gift funds is the reason we didn't send anyone home before they'd eaten. I wanted to tell you that directly. This year has been harder than the last, in the ways you would expect and a few you wouldn't. Your gift makes a specific kind of difference, which is the only kind that matters. Thank you for staying with us.

Total time: maybe four minutes. The first output would have gone in the recycling bin. The fifth output is one you would actually send.

When to start over instead of iterating

Not every bad output is worth saving. Some prompts produce drafts that drift in a direction you cannot pull back from. Two signs it is time to start a new conversation:

Signal one

After three rounds of refinement, the output is still in the same wrong register. This usually means the AI locked onto a frame in turn one that you cannot dislodge. Open a new chat with a clearer setup.

Signal two

You are making the output worse with each turn. This happens when you are correcting symptoms instead of the underlying mismatch. Step away from the chat, figure out what you actually want, and start fresh with a tighter brief.

The "tell it what to leave out" move

Most prompts tell the AI what to include. The under-used move is telling it what to leave out. Banned-word lists, banned-structure lists, banned-tone lists. These shape output more reliably than positive instructions because they prevent the laziest defaults.

An example exclusion list worth reusing

Avoid: "leverage," "navigate," "passionate," "transformative," "robust," "comprehensive," "ensure," "delve into," "unlock," "seamlessly," "uniquely positioned," "innovative approach," "in today's," "moving forward," "going forward." Avoid em dashes. Avoid three-part parallel lists unless the content genuinely needs them. Avoid opening any paragraph with "Furthermore," "Moreover," or "It is important to note."

Save this list once and paste it into every drafting prompt. The output quality lift is immediate and lasting.

Habits that produce better first drafts

Iteration is unavoidable, but you can shorten the loop with a few habits that load context into the opening prompt rather than relying on the AI to guess.

  1. State the audience in one specific sentence. "Reading-level appropriate for a busy ED who has been at the org seven years and is skeptical of buzzwords" produces a different draft than "donors."

  2. Name what success looks like. "The reader should want to keep reading past the first paragraph" or "the reader should feel respected, not pitched" gives the AI a target.

  3. Show one example of the form, even a rough one. If you can paste a paragraph that's in roughly the right register, the AI will land closer to the target on turn one.

  4. Set a length explicitly. "Around 250 words" produces tighter output than no constraint. The AI will overshoot or undershoot, but it gives you something to push against.

  5. Ask one clarifying question first. Add "Before drafting, ask me one clarifying question if any key context is missing." This works surprisingly often and prevents wasted first drafts.

The self-critique move

One of the most useful prompts in the entire library is the simplest: "Identify the three weakest sentences in what you just wrote. Tell me what is wrong with each, then rewrite them."

The AI is genuinely capable of evaluating its own output when asked. It will rarely do this unprompted, because its default is to present finished-looking text confidently. But asked directly, it will mark the filler, the clichés, and the unsupported claims, and produce a better version of those passages.

Run this on any draft before you sign off. It catches things you would have caught on a second pass yourself, with less wear.

What to actually do this week

Pick one piece of writing you would normally have produced this week (a newsletter article, a thank-you letter, a meeting recap). Draft it the way you usually would. Then take the same brief into your AI tool and iterate through at least five turns using the moves above. Compare the final outputs and decide which one you would send.

If iteration won, you now have a workflow worth keeping. If your original won, you've learned something useful about what AI is not good at in your voice. Either is a good outcome.
