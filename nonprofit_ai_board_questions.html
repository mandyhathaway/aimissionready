Five questions every nonprofit board should ask about AI

A one-page briefing for board chairs and governance committees

AI governance is a board responsibility. A February 2026 analysis from Forvis Mazars made this explicit: AI oversight now belongs inside the board's traditional fiduciary duties. This briefing gives you five questions worth asking your executive director or AI lead, with the reasoning behind each and the kind of answer that should reassure you, or worry you.

Why this is a board matter, not just a management one

The board's fiduciary duties of Care, Loyalty, and Obedience extend to technological oversight. Care means the board is responsible for understanding the material risks the organization is running; AI is now one of those. Loyalty means decisions must serve the organization's mission and the people it exists to serve, not vendor convenience or staff curiosity. Obedience means staying within the legal and regulatory framework, including HIPAA, ADA, privacy law, and state-specific obligations that apply to AI use.

Boards do not need to make tool decisions. Boards do need to confirm that someone is making them deliberately, with the right inputs, and that the resulting decisions are being documented and revisited.

Question 1
Do we have a written AI use policy, and when was it last reviewed?

The baseline. An organization without a written AI policy is operating without governance on a technology that is already being used by staff. The policy does not need to be long. It does need to exist, to be current, and to be known by the people it applies to.

What a good answer sounds like"Yes, last reviewed [recent date]. It covers what data cannot go into AI tools, who approves new tools, and a 'when not to use AI' section. Staff received training on it within the last twelve months. Next review is scheduled for [date]."
Question 2
Where is AI currently being used in our organization, and who decided?

An honest answer requires that someone has actually looked. Most nonprofits underestimate the AI use happening in their own systems because vendors have been adding AI features by default. The board is entitled to a current inventory.

What a good answer sounds like"We audited AI use across our tools in the last [timeframe]. Current AI features are enabled in [list of systems]. Each was reviewed and approved by [role or committee]. We disabled AI features in [list] because they did not meet our data terms standard."
Question 3
How would we know if an AI tool we use produced a serious error or caused harm?

The question separates organizations that have thought about AI risk from organizations that have not. A serious AI error in a nonprofit context could mean a hallucinated statistic in a funder proposal, a biased screening that affected a real person, or a privacy breach that triggered HIPAA notification obligations. The board's job is to confirm that detection mechanisms exist.

What a good answer sounds like"Staff are trained to flag AI errors to [role]. We track AI-related incidents in [system]. We have a defined response for incidents that affect clients, donors, or compliance obligations. The most recent [reporting period] had [number] flagged incidents, of which [number] required a written response."
Question 4
If a funder, a regulator, or a journalist asked us about our AI use, what would we say?

A useful stress test. The organization should be able to describe its AI use clearly, honestly, and quickly, without scrambling for talking points. If the answer is not currently ready, it should be by the end of the next board cycle. Funders are starting to ask.

What a good answer sounds like"We use AI tools for [specific uses]. Our policy is at [link]. Our data terms with each major AI vendor are [summary]. We have not had any reportable incidents in the past [period]. The board approved this approach on [date] and reviews annually."
Question 5
Who in our organization is responsible for AI decisions, and how is that role supported?

Accountability is the protection. If no one specific is responsible, no one specific is responsible. The role does not need to be a dedicated FTE. It does need to be named, supported, and resourced enough to actually do the work.

What a good answer sounds like"[Role title] is the named AI lead. They have [number] hours per [period] allocated for this work, access to [training or external support], and authority to approve or block new AI use. They report on AI matters to [board committee] on a [cadence] basis."

If your board asks these five questions and gets confident, specific answers, AI governance is in reasonable shape and your fiduciary duties are being met. If the answers are vague, partial, or absent, your board has just identified the work that needs to happen before the next meeting. Either outcome is useful.
