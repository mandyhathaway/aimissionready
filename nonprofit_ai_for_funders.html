How to ask grantees about AI use

A practical guide for program officers, foundation staff, and grantmakers
Mandy Hathaway Consulting  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics & Technology Consulting

This guide is for the other side of the grants conversation. Almost every other resource on nonprofit AI is written for the nonprofit. This one is written for the funders, the foundation program officers, and the grantmaking staff who are starting to wonder whether their portfolios are exposed to risks they have not asked about yet.

Recent sector data tells the story: by 2026, 92 percent of nonprofits report using AI in some form, while 47 percent have no AI governance policy at all. Your grantees are in that 47 percent at higher rates than you would guess. The question is not whether their AI use affects the work you fund. It already does. The question is whether your due diligence reflects that.

Funders shape what nonprofits prioritize more than any other single force in the sector. If funders ask about AI governance the way they ask about financial controls, the governance gap closes. If they do not, it does not.

Why this matters now, specifically

Three things have shifted in the last eighteen months that make AI a funder-relevant question rather than an internal nonprofit-operations question.

  1. Impact data is increasingly AI-shaped. Grant reports are being drafted with AI assistance, sometimes appropriately and sometimes not. Outcome statistics that funders rely on for board reporting may have AI synthesis between the raw data and the narrative. Without disclosure, you cannot tell.

  2. Funder reputational exposure travels through the grant. When a nonprofit you fund experiences an AI-related incident (client data exposure, biased automated decisions, a public scandal involving AI-generated materials), the funder's name is in the press alongside the grantee's. The exposure is real even when the funder did nothing wrong.

  3. Beneficiary harm from AI happens in funded work. The most serious nonprofit AI harms in 2024 and 2025 happened in funded programs: biased eligibility screening, surveillance of residential service clients, automated decision-making in benefits administration. Funders who back these programs share moral, if not legal, accountability for the systems running inside them.

The risks of asking badly

Funder questions shape grantee behavior. Bad questions produce worse outcomes than no questions at all. Three patterns to avoid.

Pattern one: bureaucratic burden disconnected from risk

Long, generic AI questionnaires sent to every grantee regardless of grant size or AI exposure. Small nonprofits spend hours generating responses no one will read. Capacity goes to compliance theater rather than service delivery.

Pattern two: questions that punish honest answers

Asking "do you have an AI policy" in a way that signals "you should." Grantees learn that the answer is yes regardless of reality. Honest "no, but we are working on it" answers become risk indicators for renewal. The result is more policies on paper, less honest reporting, and no real change.

Pattern three: chilling small nonprofit innovation

Requiring sophisticated AI governance from organizations that lack the capacity to build it. Some funders have started attaching enterprise-tier compliance language to small operating grants. The grantees that comply have someone on staff to do the compliance work, which is not most of them.

A three-tier approach proportional to risk and grant size

Match the depth of AI inquiry to the actual risk exposure of the funded work. The framework below is one approach, designed to be light at the bottom tier and substantive at the top without becoming bureaucratic in either direction.

Tier 1: Small grants and emergency funding (under $25K)

No AI-specific due diligence. The capacity cost outweighs the risk reduction at this funding level. If the grantee is using AI, your grant is not the lever that changes anything. Reserve substantive AI questions for the larger grants where the answers are likely to be actionable.

Optional addition: include a one-line statement in the grant agreement that the grantee is responsible for compliance with applicable laws including privacy and data protection.

Tier 2: Standard program grants ($25K to $250K)

A short, three-question section in the proposal or renewal form. Designed to take the grantee under thirty minutes to answer and to give your team enough signal to know whether further conversation is warranted.

  1. If your work involves AI tools, briefly describe how. If it does not, write "not applicable" and continue. (Allow the no-AI answer without penalty.)
  2. Does your organization have a written policy on AI use, and if yes, when was it last reviewed? (Honest "no, in development" is acceptable; what you are looking for is awareness.)
  3. Have you encountered any AI-related issues in the past year that affected your clients, staff, or operations? (Phrased to invite honesty about minor incidents.)
Tier 3: Major grants, multi-year commitments, and any grant funding direct-service AI ($250K and up, or any grant where AI is in the service delivery)

A full due diligence section, typically five to eight questions, that should match the scope of the grant. Grantees with reasonable AI governance can answer in under two hours. Grantees without it will learn what they need to build, which is itself a useful outcome of the grant cycle.

  1. Description of AI use across the organization, including in funded programs specifically
  2. Current written AI policy with a summary of key provisions and the date of last review
  3. Named individual responsible for AI governance, with role and reporting relationship
  4. Data-handling rules for client information, with confirmation that they are reflected in vendor contracts
  5. How decisions about new AI tools are made, and who has authority to approve or block
  6. How the organization would detect, document, and respond to an AI-related incident affecting clients
  7. For any AI in direct-service delivery: confirmation of human review, equity assessment, and beneficiary disclosure
  8. What support, training, or technical assistance would help the organization strengthen its AI governance over the grant period

What good answers look like

The answers you receive are not pass-fail. They are signals. The patterns worth weighting more heavily than the specifics:

  • Honest acknowledgment of where the organization is, including gaps
  • Evidence that someone specific has been thinking about AI, even if structures are not yet built
  • Concrete examples of decisions made, not abstract policy language
  • Awareness of beneficiary perspective in AI decision-making
  • A trajectory: where they were a year ago, where they are now, where they intend to be

Signals that warrant follow-up conversation

  • Polished AI policy language with no implementation detail or staff awareness behind it
  • "We use AI" without ability to describe how, where, or in which tools
  • AI used in eligibility or service decisions without named human-decision-making authority
  • Refusal to disclose AI use to clients or program participants
  • Vendor relationships described without ability to summarize data terms
  • No named AI lead, or the named lead has no allocated time to do the work

None of these is automatic grounds for declining a grant. All are reasons to have a longer conversation, and to consider whether your foundation can offer support, technical assistance, or a capacity-building add-on that helps the grantee close the gap.

Suggested language for your grant agreement

One paragraph that captures the substance without becoming a separate appendix. Modify for your foundation's voice and counsel.

Sample grant agreement clause

"Grantee agrees that if generative AI or other artificial intelligence tools are used in connection with the funded work, Grantee will: (a) maintain a written internal policy governing such use, including data-handling requirements and human review of consequential decisions; (b) ensure that vendors processing organizational or beneficiary data under AI features are bound by appropriate confidentiality and data-use terms; (c) disclose AI use in grant reports where AI materially contributed to deliverables or impact measurement; and (d) notify Funder within thirty days of any AI-related incident affecting beneficiaries, staff, or Grantee's compliance with applicable privacy law."

Questions worth never asking

  • "Do you use AI?" as a single yes-or-no question (almost meaningless, given how broadly AI is now embedded in commercial software)
  • "Are you HIPAA-compliant in your AI use?" (too binary, asks the grantee to make a legal certification they probably should not)
  • "Can you guarantee your AI use will not cause harm?" (no honest organization can say yes)
  • "Is your AI ethical?" (signals you want the right marketing language, not the right behavior)
  • Anything that pressures grantees toward more AI adoption rather than more deliberate AI adoption

How funders can support grantees toward responsible AI adoption

The grantee response that produces the most lasting good is not the polished policy document. It is the organization that builds real internal capacity. Funders who want to move the field forward have several levers worth considering.

  1. Capacity-building grants specifically for AI governance. Even small ones. A $5,000 grant earmarked for an AI policy review, staff training, or vendor audit can produce more durable change than a much larger general operating grant in this area.

  2. Cohort-based learning across your portfolio. Convene three to ten of your grantees for shared learning on AI governance. The peer learning is often more useful than any consultant.

  3. Pre-vetted vendor lists and templates. Save your grantees from each independently re-doing the same vendor-vetting work. Curate, share, update.

  4. Technical assistance as a renewable benefit. Include access to AI governance technical assistance in renewal terms, not just initial grants.

  5. Funder collaboration on shared standards. Coordinate with peer foundations on what you ask grantees, to reduce the duplicative-reporting burden. Many networks (PEAK Grantmaking, Council on Foundations, Technology Affinity Group) are starting to do this work.

What to do this cycle

  1. Add Tier 2 questions to your standard proposal form. Three questions, under thirty minutes for the grantee. Pilot with one program area before rolling out portfolio-wide.

  2. Identify your highest-risk grants. Any direct-service work where AI is in the loop, any grant over your foundation's defined major-grant threshold. Apply Tier 3 to these.

  3. Decide your foundation's posture on supportive vs. compliance-oriented engagement. Both are legitimate. Be explicit internally about which yours is, because grantees will read the signal regardless.

  4. Track what you learn across the portfolio. The data is itself a sector contribution. Patterns across your grantees will tell you what kinds of capacity-building support are most needed.

Funders who ask about AI thoughtfully shape what their grantees prioritize and shift the entire sector toward more deliberate adoption. Funders who ask poorly produce paper compliance and resentment. The framework above is a starting point. Adapt it for your foundation's culture, your grantee profile, and the specific risks of the work you fund.
