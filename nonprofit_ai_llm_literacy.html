Getting more from any AI

Custom GPTs, Claude Projects, Gemini Gems, and the underused art of context
Mandy Hathaway Consulting  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics & Technology Consulting

Most nonprofit staff are using maybe fifteen percent of what these AI tools can do. The other eighty-five percent is not buried in some advanced setting that requires a developer. It is sitting one click away in the menu, and it changes the output quality substantially. This guide covers what those features are, what to call them in each major tool, and how to start using them for the work you already do.

The premise: an AI tool that does not know your organization will always produce generic output. The same tool given the right context produces work that sounds like it was written by someone who knows you. That context can be set up once and reused, instead of pasted into every prompt.

The difference between "AI is interesting but the output is too generic" and "this is genuinely useful" is usually thirty minutes of setup, not a year of expertise.

The four moves that change AI output quality

Every major AI tool now supports the same four moves, even if each vendor calls them something different. Understanding the moves matters more than memorizing the vendor names.

  1. Custom instructions or system prompts. Persistent context the AI sees every time you start a conversation. "I am the development director at a homelessness services nonprofit. Default to plain language. Avoid the words 'leverage,' 'navigate,' and 'transformative.' If you are unsure of a fact, say so instead of guessing." Set once, applies to every chat.

  2. Reference documents loaded into context. Upload your style guide, your annual report, your board policy on AI, your case statement. The AI pulls from these when relevant. Same tool, infinitely better outputs, because it is now working from your actual material instead of training data alone.

  3. Worked examples. Show the AI three good donor thank-you letters you have written before. Now ask for a fourth in the same voice. This works because the AI is genuinely good at pattern-matching when you give it the pattern.

  4. Iteration on the same setup. The first output is rarely the best one. With a custom assistant set up once, iteration becomes cheap. "Make it shorter." "Less marketing-tone." "Rewrite the third paragraph." Each turn refines without losing the context.

That is the entire concept. The vendor-specific products that wrap these moves up into a saved package are below.

What each vendor calls it

Three different names for substantially the same idea: a saved AI assistant with your custom instructions and reference files baked in, ready to be reopened any time without redoing the setup.

Custom GPT

ChatGPT

Available on Plus, Team, and Enterprise tiers. Create through the "Explore GPTs" menu. Upload up to twenty reference files. Can be kept private to you, shared with your team, or published publicly.

Project

Claude

Available on Pro, Team, and Enterprise tiers. Create through the sidebar. Add reference docs and custom instructions. Each Project keeps its own conversation history separate.

Gem

Gemini

Available on Gemini Advanced and Workspace tiers. Create through the Gem manager. Add system instructions and reference behavior. Less file-upload depth than the others as of mid-2026.

A worked nonprofit example: the development team's grant assistant

Here is a concrete setup most development directors could build in under an hour.

Custom instructions to paste in

You are an assistant for the development team at [org type] serving [population] in [region]. Our voice is warm, specific, and direct. We avoid: "leverage," "transformative," "passionate," "navigate," "uniquely positioned," "innovative approach." We use first-person plural in donor communications and first-person singular in personal outreach. We always cite real numbers; if you don't have one, say so instead of inventing.

When I ask for grant content, follow the funder's stated priorities word-for-word, do not paraphrase them. When I ask for donor communications, prioritize specificity over polish. When I ask for a draft, ask me one clarifying question before drafting if any key context is missing.

Then upload as reference files: your case for support, the last three successful grant proposals (with funder names redacted if needed), your board's most recent strategic plan, a short style guide. That is the entire setup. From now on, every conversation in that assistant starts with all of that already in context.

The next time you draft a year-end appeal, you do not type "I am a development director at a homelessness services nonprofit serving 1,200 people…" because the assistant already knows. You just say what you need. The output is substantially better, immediately.

What loading documents into context actually does

The phrase you may hear is RAG, retrieval-augmented generation. The plain version: the AI looks up relevant parts of your documents at the moment you ask a question and uses them in the response, rather than relying only on what it learned during training.

The practical effect: the AI knows things specific to your organization that no public model would know. Your acronyms. Your program names. Your board's stated values. The way your founder writes. The line your last ED drew about what kinds of partnerships were off the table.

This is the single biggest move you can make. More than any prompt engineering trick, more than any new model release, the AI tool that has your real material in its context will outperform a fresh chat every time.

Three setups worth building first

  1. The development assistant. Described above. Built once, used weekly. Highest payoff for any org with a development function.

  2. The communications assistant. Reference files: your style guide, the last six newsletters, the last twelve social posts that landed well, your founder's published writing. Use for newsletters, social posts, press releases, blog drafts. Maintains voice across the team even when different people are drafting.

  3. The board materials assistant. Reference files: your last four board packets, your bylaws, your strategic plan, your risk register. Use for meeting prep, draft resolutions, agenda construction, and the inevitable "summarize the last six months for a new board member" request. Saves hours per board meeting.

Privacy: what changes and what does not

These features sit on top of the same underlying AI service, with the same underlying data terms. A Custom GPT does not magically become HIPAA-compliant because you built it. A Claude Project is not exempt from your organization's confidentiality policies because the reference files are saved.

The rule still applies

Do not upload anything to a reference-file slot that you would not paste into a chat at the same vendor tier. If your enterprise contract says inputs are not used for training, that covers the uploads too. If you are on the consumer tier, the uploads are subject to the same terms as the chat.

What does change: because the setup is reusable, accidental data leakage is also reusable. If you upload a document with sensitive content into a Custom GPT and then forget about it, that content lives there indefinitely until you actively remove it. Treat reference-file slots with the same care you treat shared drives.

When NOT to use these features

Not every workflow benefits. A one-off question does not need its own assistant. A task that genuinely needs the AI to ignore your context (testing whether something sounds biased to an outsider, for example) is worse with a heavily customized setup. And anything that requires you to upload sensitive client content into a free or consumer-tier slot should not be done at all.

The right use case is repeated work where consistency matters: communications, board materials, fundraising, internal documentation. Use the right tool for the job, not every tool for every job.

What to actually do this week

Pick one workflow your team does at least weekly. Spend thirty minutes setting up a Custom GPT, Project, or Gem for it, with the four moves above. Use it for one real task. Note what you would change. Iterate.

Within a month, you will have one or two assistants that have absorbed your organization's voice and context, and you will start to see what these tools can actually do when they know who you are.
