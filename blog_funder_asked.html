The funder asked if you use AI. Now what?

Three answers for three situations. Plus what not to say.
Mandy Hathaway  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics Consultant & Technology Specialist

Last fall, ten of the largest foundations in the country quietly stood up a $500 million pooled fund called Humanity AI. MacArthur, Ford, Mellon, Mozilla, Omidyar, Packard, Doris Duke, Lumina, Kapor, and Siegel Family Endowment. Earlier this month, the first $18 million in grants went out. The first recipients work on AI's impact on democracy, workers' rights, journalism, and education.

If you run a nonprofit and you have not been asked about AI on a grant application yet, you almost certainly will be soon. The Humanity AI announcement is the strongest signal yet that AI competence is becoming a funder expectation, not a nice-to-have. And funders rarely ask a question once. If the program officer at the Packard Foundation is now thinking about AI as a fundable question, that question is going to show up in their LOIs, their site visit guides, and their renewal conversations.

Here is the part most nonprofit leaders miss. The funder is not necessarily asking because they want you to say yes. They are asking because they want a thoughtful answer.

The funder is not testing whether you use AI. They are testing whether you have thought about whether you should.

Why funders are asking now

Three things are happening at the same time. Foundations are taking AI seriously as a sector-wide issue, partly because their own grantees are reporting harms, surprises, and policy questions they were not prepared for. The 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report from Virtuous shows 92% of nonprofits are using AI in some form, but only 7% report major impact, and 76% have no governance policy. Funders are reading that report too. They are concerned about both the missed potential and the under-managed risk.

Regulators are also moving. The EU AI Act becomes fully applicable on August 2, 2026. Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act went into effect in January. California has four new AI laws on the books and an executive order from the governor that affects state contractors. Funders that operate across multiple states are starting to ask grantees whether their AI use creates compliance exposure that could surface during reporting.

And the legal landscape is unsettled. The Meta/Llama lawsuit filed this month by Elsevier, Macmillan, Hachette, McGraw Hill, and Scott Turow is the largest training-data case yet. Anthropic settled a similar suit for $1.5 billion in September. Foundations are aware that the tools they are funding their grantees to use are at the center of an active legal fight. They want grantees who have thought about that.

What the question really means

When a funder asks "does your organization use AI," they are usually testing for three things at once.

First, they want to know if you are paying attention. A nonprofit that says "we have not thought about it" in May 2026 raises a flag, not because using AI is required, but because not thinking about it is hard to defend. The tools are in every Microsoft and Google product your organization already uses. Saying you have not thought about AI is, at this point, close to saying you have not thought about your operational technology stack.

Second, they want to know if you are honest. The fastest way to lose a funder's trust is to claim AI use that does not exist, or to claim AI competence that does not exist. Program officers talk to each other, and they read your other reports, and they can usually tell when the language in the AI paragraph does not match the language anywhere else in the application.

Third, they want to know if your answer is consistent with the rest of your application. If you are claiming AI-powered impact while reporting client-data work in a regulated sector, the funder will want to understand how you are reconciling those things. If you say you do not use AI but your grant materials include outputs that obviously involved AI, the inconsistency is the problem.

Three scenarios, three honest answers

Below are three scenarios that cover most nonprofits, plus the language I would use in a grant application for each. These are not templates to paste. They are starting points to adapt.

Scenario 1

Your organization does not use AI in any meaningful way

This is rarer than it used to be, but it is still real, especially for smaller nonprofits with stable workflows and no recent technology turnover. The honest answer is to say so, and to say why.

"Our organization has not adopted AI tools for client-facing work or internal operations beyond features built into our existing software (Microsoft 365, Google Workspace). The board has not yet made a formal decision about broader AI adoption. We are tracking the question and expect to revisit it in [timeframe], with particular attention to client data protections and staff capacity."

What this answer does well: it acknowledges that AI features exist in tools you are already using, it names the absence of a formal decision, and it gives the funder a timeframe. It does not pretend to be more advanced than you are.

Scenario 2

Staff use AI informally and there is no written policy

This is the most common situation in May 2026. The honest answer is to say so, and to describe what you are doing about it.

"Staff use general-purpose AI tools (such as ChatGPT and the AI features built into Microsoft 365) for tasks including writing assistance, document drafting, and research. We do not currently have a formal AI use policy. We are in the process of developing one, with a target completion date of [date]. In the interim, our [executive director or designated person] has issued internal guidance covering the categories of information that may not be entered into any external AI tool."

What this answer does well: it tells the truth about informal use, names a specific accountable person, gives a concrete date, and describes interim measures. It does not hide what is happening, and it does not promise more than you can deliver.

Scenario 3

Your organization has an AI use policy and is operating under it

This is still uncommon but increasingly possible. If you are here, the answer is short and factual.

"Our organization has a written AI use policy, adopted in [month/year], covering staff use of generative AI tools. The policy specifies approved tools, prohibited data categories, vendor evaluation requirements, and incident reporting procedures. It is reviewed [quarterly/semiannually] by our [ED, board committee, or designated lead]. We can provide a copy to the funder on request."

What this answer does well: it states the facts, identifies the accountable person, and offers the document. It does not oversell. Funders trust short factual answers more than long aspirational ones.

What not to say

A few patterns reliably hurt grant applications in this category.

Do not claim AI use you do not actually have. If your organization has not adopted AI tools, do not write a paragraph implying that you have. Funders read enough applications to recognize aspirational language pretending to be factual language, and the credibility hit travels with you.

Do not promise an AI policy in 30 days if it will take six months. Pick a date you can actually meet, then meet it. A missed deliverable on something the funder is now paying attention to is worse than a more honest timeline at the application stage.

Do not name specific tools by brand unless you have read their terms of service. If you write "we use ChatGPT Enterprise" in a grant application and you are actually using the free tier, the funder may not catch it, but a savvy program officer will, and the gap between what you said and what you do is the kind of detail that ends conversations.

Do not treat the AI question as a marketing opportunity. The funder is not asking you to demonstrate enthusiasm. They are asking for an accurate accounting. Save the enthusiasm for the impact narrative.

The funder is asking because they want to fund you well

I do not think most program officers are trying to catch grantees out on this question. The people I have talked to at foundations are asking about AI for the same reason they ask about data security and staff turnover: because problems in these areas tend to surface as problems in grant performance later, and they would rather understand the situation now than find out about it during a reporting cycle.

The good news is that an honest answer, even one that says "we are not there yet," is almost always better received than a polished one that overclaims. If your organization is in the 76% with no policy, the funder is not surprised. They are reading the same reports you are. What they want to see is that you know where you stand and that someone is paying attention.

The Humanity AI announcement is the first wave. The state laws and the EU deadline are the second. By the time you are writing a grant application in 2027, the AI question will be standard. Worth getting your answer ready now.
