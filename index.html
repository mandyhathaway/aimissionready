About this site

Why this exists

My background is in nonprofits. I spent years doing work where the point was helping people, not building revenue, and that shaped how I think about everything that came after, including AI.

I'm an AI ethics specialist. That phrase covers more ground than it might suggest: bias and fairness, privacy and consent, labor impacts, environmental cost, accessibility, power concentration, democratic accountability, cultural sensitivity, and more. At the core of all of it is one question: does this help people, animals, or the planet, or does it extract from them? I work only on projects where the answer is a clear yes.

The people doing the most good in the world need to understand these tools. Not because they should love them, but because those tools are being used around them, and against the communities they serve, whether they engage with that reality or not.

Creating and curating these resources is my contribution to changing that.

Before you go further

Common questions

Six things people ask before downloading the first guide. Short answers, no fine print.

Is everything here really free?

Yes. Every guide is free to download. No email required, no account, no paywall. The external resource library is also free, though some of the linked organizations charge for advanced training or certification programs. Those are noted on the cards.

Who is behind this site?

I am. My name is Mandy Hathaway. I am an AI ethics consultant and technical writer with a master's degree in Ethical Technology and Artificial Intelligence. I built this hub because most of the AI guidance I could find for nonprofits was either too abstract or too vendor-backed. More on the About page.

Where should I start if I'm new to this?

If your organization has not yet decided whether to use AI, start with the Considering AI lane. If your staff are already using AI tools, the Already Using AI lane is the right entry point. The data security guide is a reasonable first read for anyone in either situation.

Can I share these with my board or staff?

Yes, please do. The guides are licensed under Creative Commons BY-NC 4.0, which means you can share them, print them, and use them internally with attribution. The only thing you cannot do is sell them or repackage them as a commercial product.

Are you selling something?

Not on this site. I do run a consulting practice at mandyhathaway.com that offers AI ethics work for nonprofits, small businesses, and teams building ethical AI systems. If the guides are useful and you want more personalized help, that is where to find it. There is no upsell embedded in any of the resources here.

What if I have a question that isn't covered?

Send it. There is a suggestion form on the About page, or you can email me directly. If enough people are asking the same question, it becomes the next guide or blog post.

About this collection

Why these guides exist

Most nonprofit AI advice is written for people who already speak the language. These guides are written for the person whose actual job is something else, and who got handed AI as one more thing to figure out.

Every guide is practitioner-written, plain-language, and free. No paywalls, no vendor backing, no upsell. You can read them in your browser or download them. You can share them with your team, your board, or a peer organization without asking permission. Use them, adapt them, copy from them.

Two ways to browse: by topic (governance, data, vendors, staff, equity, sector) when you know what you're working on, or by role (executive director, program staff, board, funder, the tech-adjacent person) when you want to see what other people in your seat are reading. Frequently asked

Ten questions people ask about AI

The questions that come up most often in conversations with nonprofit leaders. Short answers, no fence-sitting.

What is "AI" really, in 2026?

In most current conversations, AI means a large language model: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and similar. These models predict the next likely piece of text based on patterns from training data. They are not databases, not search engines, and not reasoning systems in the way humans reason. They are very good pattern matchers that produce fluent output.

Why does AI confidently say things that are wrong?

Because it is predicting what fluent text looks like, not checking whether the content is true. The technical term is hallucination. Treat any factual claim from AI as an unverified draft. Verify before you act on it, especially names, numbers, citations, and laws.

Is anything I type into ChatGPT private?

By default, no. Free and consumer-tier AI tools typically reserve the right to use what you type to improve their models. Enterprise and team tiers usually have stronger commitments. Read the terms before you paste anything sensitive. See the privacy guide for the actual rules per major tool.

Can I put client information into AI?

Not into a consumer chatbot. Not without an enterprise contract that says your inputs are not used for training. Not for any client with sensitive case details, protected health information, or anything covered by a confidentiality or privilege rule. Even when allowed, anonymize where you can.

Will AI replace my staff?

Probably not in the way the hype suggests. AI replaces specific tasks more than whole roles, and the tasks it replaces are usually the parts of jobs people already wish they could offload. The bigger risk is not replacement, it is the "impact treadmill": expectations rising to match new capacity without staff getting any of that capacity back as breathing room.

What does AI cost?

Less than you might expect for individual use. A team subscription to ChatGPT or Claude runs about $25 to $60 per user per month. Enterprise tiers and specialized vendor tools cost more. The hidden costs are staff time to evaluate tools, training, and the cleanup work after the first time something goes wrong.

Is AI biased?

Yes, in measurable and well-documented ways. Models learn patterns from training data that reflects historical and ongoing discrimination. Outputs reproduce those patterns unless deliberately mitigated. The bias is most dangerous when AI is used to make or shape decisions about people, especially benefits, eligibility, hiring, or risk scores.

What's the environmental cost?

Real and growing. Training large models takes enormous amounts of energy and water. Day-to-day use is smaller per query but adds up quickly at scale. For organizations whose mission centers climate or justice, this is worth knowing and worth raising with vendors.

Do I have to have an AI policy?

Yes, if any of your staff use AI, which they almost certainly do. A short, clear policy is better than a long, thorough one no one reads. Start with the staff use policy guide in the library. The minimum: what cannot go into AI, who approves new tools, and where to ask questions.

How fast is this changing?

Fast enough that any policy you write needs an explicit review date. Slow enough that the fundamentals (data hygiene, human review of important decisions, transparency about AI use) have been stable for years. Build for the fundamentals first.

What can go wrong

Eight risks worth knowing about

Not the doomsday list. The actual operational risks that show up in nonprofit AI use.

Risk 1

Hallucination

The model invents facts, citations, names, or numbers that sound plausible. Most dangerous when the output is then sent to funders, clients, or boards without verification.

Risk 2

Data leakage

Staff paste client information, donor records, or financial details into free AI tools. Some of those tools train on inputs. Sensitive content ends up in training data or system logs you cannot retrieve.

Risk 3

Bias in outputs

The model reproduces patterns from biased training data. Particularly dangerous in any tool used to score, screen, prioritize, or make decisions about people.

Risk 4

Vendor lock-in

An AI tool gets baked into a critical workflow. The vendor changes pricing, deprecates the feature, or gets acquired. Migrating away costs more than the original adoption did.

Risk 5

Default-on AI features

Existing vendor (EHR, CRM, project management, video) adds AI features and turns them on by default. Sensitive data flows into the AI feature without anyone opting in.

Risk 6

Shadow AI

Staff use AI tools without telling anyone. Risk is unmanaged because leadership does not know it exists. Less about staff intent than about an organization that has not made it safe to ask.

Risk 7

Automation in high-stakes decisions

AI used to determine eligibility, prioritize cases, or shape benefits decisions. Errors compound, harms fall hardest on the most vulnerable, and accountability gets diffused across humans and the system.

Risk 8

Reputation damage from disclosure

A client, funder, or journalist discovers that AI was used in something they thought was human-written or human-decided. The credibility hit is often larger than the underlying mistake.

Plain-language glossary

Eighteen terms worth knowing

The vocabulary you'll encounter in vendor pitches, funder questions, and board conversations. Short definitions, no jargon used to explain jargon.

AIAn umbrella term. In 2026 it usually means a large language model that produces text or code.
LLM (large language model)A model trained on huge amounts of text that predicts the next likely word. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini are all LLMs.
PromptThe text you give an AI tool to get a useful response. A good prompt has context, task, constraints, and any input materials.
TokenThe unit AI tools count. Roughly four characters or three-quarters of a word in English. Pricing is per token.
Context windowHow much text the AI can hold in mind at once. Bigger context means you can paste in more material before the AI starts forgetting.
HallucinationWhen the AI confidently produces something that is wrong. The technical term for output that sounds true but is not.
Training dataThe text the model learned from. Includes most of the public internet plus licensed books, code, and other sources. Sometimes includes user inputs.
Fine-tuningTaking an existing model and further training it on specific data so it performs better on a narrow task.
RAG (retrieval-augmented generation)When the AI looks up information from your documents at the moment of the prompt rather than relying only on training data.
Custom GPT / Project / GemPer-vendor names for a customized AI assistant that has been set up with specific instructions and reference files.
AgentAn AI that takes actions in the world (sends emails, browses the web, runs code) instead of just producing text in response.
MultimodalAn AI that handles more than just text: images, audio, video, code, all in the same model.
APIA way for one piece of software to talk to another. When a vendor "uses AI," they usually call an AI provider's API behind the scenes.
Enterprise tierThe paid plan with stronger commitments: typically no training on your data, better admin controls, and a contract you can negotiate.
BAA (Business Associate Agreement)The HIPAA contract that allows a vendor to handle protected health information on your behalf. Required before any HIPAA-covered data goes to AI.
SubprocessorA vendor that your vendor uses. Matters because your data flows through them too. Always ask for the list.
Shadow AIStaff using AI tools without telling anyone. Usually not malicious. Almost always present.
Human-in-the-loopA required human review step in an AI workflow. The thing that prevents an AI error from becoming an AI-caused harm.

Honest reviews

AI Tools

The tools your team is likely to encounter, with honest takes on strengths, weaknesses, and where the nonprofit-specific catches live. No affiliate links, no vendor relationships, no upsell.

Before any tool gets on the list

Red flags worth catching early

Vendors come and go. Tools change policies. Privacy terms get updated quietly. Rather than name and shame specific tools (which goes stale fast), here is the rubric we apply when reviewing anything. If a tool fails on more than one of these, it does not make our list. If it fails on the first two, it should not be approved for staff use anywhere.

1. Trains on your inputs by default.If the only way to stop training is to be on the enterprise tier, the free and team tiers are not safe for any sensitive content.
2. No BAA available at any tier.Disqualifying for HIPAA-covered nonprofits, full stop. No workaround.
3. No SOC 2 Type II or equivalent.Standard baseline for any vendor that handles organizational data. If the vendor cannot produce one, ask why.
4. Unclear data residency or subprocessor list.If you cannot find out where your data is processed and which third parties touch it, you cannot tell your funders or clients either.
5. No enterprise tier with stronger commitments.If there is no path to a contract with real teeth, the tool is fine for personal use but not for the organization.
6. Track record of incidents or rapid policy reversals.Pattern matters more than any single incident. A vendor that quietly tightens its terms after pushback is signaling something about how it makes decisions.
7. Owned or controlled by an entity with hostile interests.Particularly relevant if your organization serves communities targeted by the owner's public positions. Worth raising in board conversations even when other factors check out.
8. Marketing leans hard on "ethical AI" with no specifics.Real commitments show up in terms, policies, and audits. Vague "we care about ethics" copy with nothing behind it is a tell.

Currently a no from us, for the reasons above: Grok (xAI) by default trains on user inputs and operates with notably less external accountability than the other major providers. We do not include it in any category on this list. If their policies meaningfully change, we will revisit. Checked: May 2026.

Conversational AI

The chat tools your staff are most likely using already. Use case overlap is real, but data terms and admin controls vary widely.

Claude

Anthropic

Good for: careful reasoning, long-document analysis, drafting that requires nuance, plain-language explanation. Stronger than most at refusing to do things it shouldn't.

Watch out for: consumer tier (claude.ai free, Pro) does not include enterprise data protections. Use the Team or Enterprise tier or API with no-training terms for any organizational data.

Nonprofit notes: no public-facing nonprofit discount as of this writing. Worth contacting Anthropic directly if you are a mission-aligned nonprofit looking for Team access.

ChatGPT

OpenAI

Good for: the broadest range of general tasks. Largest ecosystem of custom GPTs, plugins, and integrations. Most familiar to staff already.

Watch out for: the free tier and Plus tier have weaker data terms than Team and Enterprise. Custom GPTs published publicly can leak the instructions and reference files you load into them.

Nonprofit notes: OpenAI for Nonprofits offers ChatGPT Team at a discount. Eligibility verified through Percent. Worth applying.

Gemini

Google

Good for: orgs already in Google Workspace. Strong integration with Docs, Sheets, Gmail. Multimodal handling of images and video.

Watch out for: the free tier of Gemini is not subject to the same data terms as Workspace-integrated Gemini. Make sure staff are using the Workspace-integrated version when handling org data.

Nonprofit notes: available at discount through Google for Nonprofits. If your org already runs on Workspace, this is the path of least resistance.

Microsoft Copilot

Microsoft

Good for: orgs already in Microsoft 365. Tight integration with Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams. Strong admin controls on the enterprise side.

Watch out for: the free, web-based Copilot is not the same product as Copilot inside your 365 tenant. Different data terms, different controls. Train staff on which one they should be using.

Nonprofit notes: discounted through Microsoft for Nonprofits. Often comes bundled with discounted 365 licenses.

Perplexity

Perplexity AI

Good for: research tasks where you want sources, not just an answer. The closest current substitute for "use search and synthesize" workflows.

Watch out for: sources can still be misrepresented in the synthesis. Always click through to the citation before quoting.

Nonprofit notes: no broadly publicized nonprofit pricing. Free tier is workable for occasional use, paid Pro tier is reasonable for staff who research heavily.

What we don't recommend in this category: Grok (xAI), per the red-flags note above. For free-tier conversational use we suggest Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini, all of which have stronger default protections and clearer escalation paths.

Office & productivity

AI features baked into the tools your team already uses every day. Often the highest-payoff starting point because the integration overhead is zero.

Microsoft 365 Copilot

Microsoft

Good for: drafting in Word, formulas and data analysis in Excel, email triage in Outlook, meeting summaries in Teams. The deepest integration available in the Microsoft world.

Watch out for: can summarize sensitive content in meeting recaps if no one configures otherwise. Review default settings on Teams recording and transcription before broad rollout.

Nonprofit notes: discounted with Microsoft for Nonprofits. Costs less per seat than the standard commercial price.

Google Workspace Gemini

Google

Good for: the Google-ecosystem equivalent of 365 Copilot. Drafting in Docs, formulas in Sheets, summarizing in Gmail, smart canvas in Meet.

Watch out for: Workspace admin controls for Gemini are still maturing. Audit which staff have access to what before assuming defaults are safe.

Nonprofit notes: available through Google for Nonprofits. If you are already on Workspace, this is the integration you want.

NotebookLM

Google

Good for: giving the AI a specific set of documents to work from rather than relying on training data. Genuinely useful for research, policy synthesis, and grant prep. Stronger guardrails than open chat because the AI is constrained to your sources.

Watch out for: still a Google product running on Google infrastructure. Same data terms apply. Do not paste in protected health information without checking your tier.

Nonprofit notes: the audio overviews are a memorable demo but the real value is the document grounding. Worth a serious look for orgs that work with lots of reference material.

Grammarly

Grammarly

Good for: writing assistance across email, documents, and the browser. Tone suggestions, grammar fixes, generative drafting on the paid tier.

Watch out for: the Grammarly browser extension sees every text field your staff type into. Make sure the deployment is the business tier with appropriate data terms, not the consumer tier installed quietly.

Nonprofit notes: Grammarly offers a nonprofit discount on the business tier. Worth using over the consumer tier for any team deployment.

Otter.ai

Otter

Good for: live transcription of meetings, with AI summary and action item extraction. Often joins your calls automatically once invited.

Watch out for: auto-joining recording bots can show up in meetings where participants did not consent. Several news stories about this in 2024 and 2025. Configure deliberately or use the meeting-host-only mode.

Nonprofit notes: no broadly publicized nonprofit discount. Consider whether your existing video conferencing platform's built-in transcription is sufficient before adding another vendor.

What we don't recommend in this category: Standalone consumer-tier AI productivity add-ins that ask for full document access without a clear data terms link. If you cannot find the terms in two clicks, do not install it on a staff computer.

Synthesia

How to use this library

A prompt is the text you give an AI tool to get a useful response. A good prompt has four parts: context (who you are and what you are doing), task (what you want the AI to produce), constraints (length, tone, format), and any examples or input materials. Almost every poor AI output traces back to a prompt that left one of these out.

Each prompt below is a starting template. The bracketed placeholders are where you fill in your own context. After you paste the AI's first response, ask follow-up questions to refine. "Make it shorter," "more direct," "less marketing-tone," "rewrite the second paragraph" are all valid next moves.

Before you use any prompt: the data hygiene rules

These prompts work with any AI tool, but they assume you are not pasting protected information into a consumer chatbot. The categories below should never be entered into any external AI tool unless you are on an enterprise plan with no-training data terms and your organization has approved that specific use:

› Client names combined with any service detail, diagnosis, or case information
› Anything from a protected health record (HIPAA-covered material)
› SSNs, financial account numbers, immigration status
› Donor names combined with giving amounts or contact details
› Anything covered by an attorney-client, counselor, or clergy privilege

Where a prompt below benefits from real context (a specific donor, a specific case), use anonymized placeholders ("a donor who has given for five years," "a client with [program type] need") rather than actual identifying details.

The four-part prompt structure

If you want to write your own prompts, not just use the templates, this is the structure that works:

1. Context. Who you are and what you are doing. ("I am a development director at a homelessness services nonprofit serving 1,200 people a year in Minneapolis.")
2. Task. What you want the AI to produce. ("Draft a four-paragraph year-end appeal letter focused on housing stability.")
3. Constraints. Length, tone, format, things to avoid. ("Around 350 words. Warm but not saccharine. No metaphors about journeys or bridges.")
4. Input or example. Any materials the AI should work from. ("Use these three program highlights: ___, ___, ___.")

Donor & fundraising communications

Letters, appeals, and outreach. These prompts assume you are working from anonymized donor profiles, not pasting in actual contact records.

1. Donor thank-you letter draft

For: drafting a personalized acknowledgment after a meaningful gift.

I am a development director at [organization type] serving [mission area]. A donor just gave [gift size category, e.g., "a mid-level gift"]. They have been giving for [time period]. Their interest area is [program or value].

Draft a three-paragraph thank-you letter that:
- Opens with specific gratitude (not generic)
- Names one concrete thing their gift will support
- Closes with an invitation to stay connected (not a request for more)

Tone: warm, conversational, like a leader speaking directly. Length: 200 to 250 words. No phrases like "your generous support" or "transformative impact."
Before you paste: Do not include the donor's name, contact info, or exact gift amount. Use category descriptors.
Pro tip: After the first draft, ask: "Make it sound less like a fundraising appeal and more like a personal note."

2. Grant letter of inquiry opening

For: drafting the first two paragraphs of an LOI to a foundation.

I am applying for a grant from [foundation type, e.g., "a regional family foundation"] with a stated interest in [their funding focus]. Our organization works on [our work, 1-2 sentences]. The specific request will be [grant amount and project].

Draft two opening paragraphs for the LOI that:
- Establish credibility without over-claiming
- Show alignment with the funder's stated interest
- Lead naturally into a specific ask

Tone: confident, specific, not promotional. No phrases like "uniquely positioned" or "innovative approach."
Before you paste: Avoid pasting confidential financial details or program data not already public.
Pro tip: Paste the funder's actual mission statement as input to improve alignment.

3. Major gift outreach email

For: a personalized email to a prospective major donor before a meeting.

I am the [your role] at [organization]. I am writing to a prospective major donor who has shown interest in [mission area] through [how they came to your attention]. We have not yet met. I want to invite them to a 30-minute conversation.

Draft a short email (under 150 words) that:
- Acknowledges the connection or referral without dwelling
- States one specific thing about our work that aligns with their stated interest
- Asks for a 30-minute conversation with two date options

Tone: warm peer-to-peer, not pitch-style. Avoid words like "partner" and "leverage."
Before you paste: No donor name, no employer, no financial detail. Reference category only.
Pro tip: Ask for three versions in different opening styles, then pick the one that fits the donor's voice profile.

4. Year-end appeal hook

For: generating opening hook options for an annual appeal letter.

I am writing the year-end appeal for [organization], a [mission type] nonprofit. This year's defining moment was [one specific story or development]. The appeal goes to [donor audience description].

Generate five different opening sentences for the letter. Each should:
- Open with a concrete moment, image, or observation
- Avoid clichés ("As the year comes to a close...")
- Lead the reader to want to keep reading

Vary the rhetorical approach: one anecdote, one provocative question, one direct address, one quiet observation, one piece of news.
Before you paste: If the defining moment involves a real person, describe in general terms without identifying details.
Pro tip: Read all five out loud. The one you instinctively want to keep reading is usually the one to use.

5. Lapsed donor re-engagement

For: an email to donors who have not given in 18 to 36 months.

I am writing to a lapsed donor (last gift 18 to 36 months ago). They previously supported [program or campaign]. Our organization has changed in [one or two specific ways] since their last gift.

Draft a short re-engagement email (under 180 words) that:
- Does not guilt-trip or open with "we miss you"
- Acknowledges the lapse without belaboring it
- Names one concrete development they would not have heard about
- Offers a specific, low-effort way back in (not necessarily a gift)

Tone: respectful of their time, not desperate.
Before you paste: No donor name, no specific gift history. General categories only.
Pro tip: The "low-effort way back in" can be a sign-up for the newsletter, a free event, or a survey. Not always a donation ask.

External communications

Newsletters, social media, press, events. The work that goes out to the broader public.

6. Newsletter article draft

For: turning a rough idea into a 400-word newsletter article.

I am writing a newsletter article for [organization], audience [donors / community / both]. The topic is [one-sentence summary]. Key points I want to cover: [bullet list of 3 to 5 points].

Draft a 400-word article that:
- Opens with a hook (concrete moment, question, or surprising fact)
- Covers the key points in a logical order, not a bullet list
- Closes with a call to action that fits the audience

Tone: clear, warm, no jargon. Avoid AI-stilted phrases.
Before you paste: If any of the key points reference clients, use anonymized descriptors.
Pro tip: Follow up with: "Cut anything that sounds like marketing copy. Make every sentence earn its place."

7. Three social media post variants

For: generating multiple variations of the same message for different platforms.

I want to share a piece of news from [organization] across social media. The news is: [1-2 sentence summary]. The action I want followers to take is [click a link, attend an event, etc.].

Generate three different social media post variants:
- One for LinkedIn (professional, slightly longer, no emojis)
- One for Facebook (warm, community-oriented, may use one emoji)
- One for Instagram caption (visual-supportive, conversational)

Each should be standalone and not require the others. No hashtag stuffing.
Before you paste: Public news only. Do not include unannounced grants, hires, or partnerships.
Pro tip: Add: "Suggest three hashtags maximum per post, ones the audience actually uses."

8. Plain-language explainer for your community

For: turning a complex policy or program change into something your clients can read.

I need to explain [topic: a policy change, a program update, a regulatory shift] to [audience: clients, community members, families]. The current explanation is full of [jargon source: legal, medical, bureaucratic].

Here is the original text: [paste source text]

Rewrite at a 6th-grade reading level. Keep the meaning accurate. Use short sentences. Avoid bullet lists unless they genuinely help. Define any term that has to stay technical.
Before you paste: Make sure the source text is public-facing material, not internal documentation or anything client-specific.
Pro tip: Read it aloud after. If you stumble on a sentence, it needs another pass.

9. Press release skeleton

For: a structural draft of a press release before you write the real version.

I am drafting a press release for [organization] announcing [news: program launch, leadership change, milestone]. Key facts: [date, location, names of people quotable, scale or impact].

Produce a structural skeleton with:
- A direct headline (no clever wordplay)
- A dateline and lead paragraph that answers who/what/when/where
- A 3-paragraph body with the supporting context
- A quote placeholder with a note on whose voice should fill it
- A boilerplate paragraph about the organization

Tone: factual, journalistic. AP style. No marketing language.
Before you paste: Confirm what is on the record. Embargoed news should not go into a chatbot.
Pro tip: Write your own quote. AI quotes always sound like AI quotes.

10. Event invitation copy

For: an event invitation email that does not sound like a marketing template.

I am inviting [audience] to [event type and topic] on [date, time, location or format]. The reason to come is [the actual value, not just "join us"].

Draft a short invitation email (under 200 words) that:
- Opens with the reason to come, not the date
- States the practical details concisely
- Closes with a clear way to RSVP

No phrases like "you are cordially invited" or "we hope you'll join us for an evening of."
Before you paste: Public event details only. Do not paste guest lists.
Pro tip: Subject lines: ask for five options that each promise something specific.

Internal operations

Meeting work, hiring, onboarding, internal communication. The behind-the-scenes work that quietly absorbs most staff hours.

11. Meeting agenda from rough notes

For: turning a list of topics into a structured 60-minute agenda.

I am leading a [meeting type: staff, board committee, partner check-in] on [date]. Duration: 60 minutes. Attendees: [roles, not names]. Topics to cover (rough order): [paste your list of topics with rough time estimates]

Produce an agenda with:
- Time allocations that add up to 60 minutes (build in 5 minutes buffer)
- A clear owner for each item
- A note on which items need a decision vs. which are informational

Format as a simple list, not a complex table.
Before you paste: Use roles, not personnel names. Avoid specifics on contested or sensitive items.
Pro tip: Ask AI to flag any item that seems under-allocated for the work involved.

12. Meeting recap from notes

For: turning your messy meeting notes into a clean recap email.

Here are my notes from a [meeting type] meeting: [paste notes]

Produce a structured recap with:
- A 2-sentence summary at the top
- Decisions made (if any)
- Action items with owners and rough due dates
- Open questions or items deferred

Keep it under 250 words total. Skip filler.
Before you paste: Strip personnel matters, salary discussions, and any individual performance content from your notes first.
Pro tip: If your notes are too thin, ask for a follow-up question list instead of a recap.

13. Job description draft

For: a first-pass job description that is not full of cliché phrases.

I am hiring a [role title] at [organization type]. The role will [3 to 5 specific responsibilities]. Compensation: [range]. Reports to: [role title]. We particularly want to attract [desired candidate profile, e.g., "career-changers from adjacent fields"].

Draft a job description that:
- Opens with the actual work, not the org backstory
- Names the salary range clearly near the top
- Describes responsibilities in plain language, not corporate-speak
- Lists genuinely necessary qualifications, not aspirational ones

Avoid words like "passionate," "rockstar," "self-starter," "fast-paced."
Before you paste: No internal salary band conflicts or candidate names from the active pool.
Pro tip: Add: "Flag any required qualification that is more habit than necessity, so I can decide whether to keep it."

14. Onboarding checklist for a role

For: a structured first-30-days checklist for a new hire.

I am onboarding a [role title] at [organization type]. The role primarily does [main responsibilities]. Direct manager is [role title]. Key systems they will use: [CRM, document store, comms tools].

Produce a 30-day onboarding checklist organized by week:
- Week 1: setup, introductions, observe
- Week 2: shadow specific people on specific tasks
- Week 3: produce first real work product (specify what)
- Week 4: feedback check-in, broader integration

Be specific. Generic onboarding lists are not useful.
Before you paste: No internal personnel notes or organizational chart specifics.
Pro tip: Ask: "What is one thing in this checklist new hires consistently report wishing they had done sooner?"

15. Policy or process explainer email

For: explaining a new internal policy to staff without sounding like compliance training.

I need to announce a new internal policy to [audience: all staff, program team, leadership]. The policy is: [brief summary]. The reason for the change is: [why it is happening]. The effective date is: [date].

Draft a short email (under 250 words) that:
- Leads with the change itself, not the reasoning
- Explains the why in one paragraph
- Names what staff need to do differently, specifically
- Closes with where to ask questions

Tone: clear, respectful, not defensive or apologetic.
Before you paste: If the policy involves personnel decisions, do not paste the underlying rationale documents.
Pro tip: Ask AI to identify any sentence that sounds like it is hiding something.

Program & client-facing materials

Workshop content, trainings, handouts, plain-language guides. Materials that go to the people you serve.

16. Plain-language version of a complex policy

For: turning legal, regulatory, or technical language into something clients can actually read.

Here is a policy or regulation: [paste public-facing source text]

Rewrite it at a 6th-grade reading level for an audience of [client population description]. Keep all the meaning accurate. Use short sentences and active voice. Define any term that must stay technical. Where the original is ambiguous, name the ambiguity rather than guess.
Before you paste: Source text should be publicly available. Do not paste internal interpretations or memos.
Pro tip: After the rewrite, ask: "What did you have to drop, and what should I tell readers to ask about?"

17. Workshop facilitation outline

For: a structured 90-minute workshop outline on a specific topic.

I am facilitating a 90-minute workshop on [topic] for [audience: 12 to 20 people, mix of staff and community members]. Their experience level is [novice / mixed / advanced]. The desired outcome is [what participants should be able to do or decide after].

Produce a facilitation outline with:
- Opening (5 min): how to set the room
- Three working segments with timing
- One small-group activity with discussion prompts
- A closing (10 min) that captures commitments

Note where energy will likely dip and what to do about it.
Before you paste: No participant names or organizational specifics from past sessions.
Pro tip: Ask for "the most likely way this workshop falls apart" as a separate question.

18. Resource handout for a topic

For: a one-page resource sheet with curated external links and brief context.

I need a one-page handout for [audience] on [topic]. Reading level: 8th grade. The handout should help readers [specific action or decision].

Produce a one-page structure with:
- A two-sentence opening that names the problem
- 3 to 5 categories of information, each with a brief explanation
- A "where to go for help" section with placeholder text for hotlines or services
- A "questions to ask yourself" closer with 3 questions

Do not invent specific organizations or hotlines. Use placeholder text where local resources should go.
Before you paste: General topic only. No client-specific details from real cases.
Pro tip: Have a staff member from the audience-adjacent role do a final read for plain language.

19. Training session structure

For: structuring a 2-hour staff training session.

I am leading a 2-hour staff training on [topic] for [role types]. Their starting knowledge is [level]. After the training, they should be able to [specific capability or knowledge].

Produce a structured outline with:
- 10-min opening (frame the why)
- Two 30-min teaching segments with built-in practice
- One 20-min applied scenario or roleplay
- 15-min Q&A and integration
- 5-min commitments and close

Note where the training risks losing the audience and how to bring it back.
Before you paste: No staff names. Use role descriptors.
Pro tip: Build in a "I'm lost" check at the 45-minute mark. Trainings that skip this overestimate how much landed.

20. Curriculum module outline

For: scoping out a multi-session curriculum on a topic.

I am designing a [number]-session curriculum on [topic] for [audience]. Each session is [length]. The overall goal is [capability or transformation desired].

Produce a module-by-module outline with:
- Title and learning goal for each session
- Brief description of how it builds on the previous one
- One signature activity per session
- Assessment or check-in moments

Flag any session that feels light or that should be split into two.
Before you paste: Course-design context only. No participant data.
Pro tip: Ask: "What is the most common reason this kind of curriculum fails to land?"

Strategy & planning

Board-level and senior staff work. These prompts produce scaffolds you refine with your team, not finished documents.

21. SWOT analysis frame

For: a starting-point SWOT to bring to a board or leadership conversation.

I am preparing a SWOT analysis for [organization]. Context: [recent developments, market conditions, internal changes]. Our mission is [brief]. We are at a [decision point or strategic moment].

Produce a draft SWOT with 4 to 6 items in each quadrant. For each, write one sentence explaining why it belongs there. Flag any item that could plausibly belong in two quadrants. Do not make up specific statistics. Use placeholders where data should go.
Before you paste: Public context only. No financial details, board dynamics, or personnel issues.
Pro tip: The SWOT items that initially seem obvious are usually wrong. Bring AI's draft to your team as a starting argument, not an answer.

22. Stakeholder mapping

For: a stakeholder map for a new initiative or decision.

I am planning [initiative or decision]. The stakeholders I have identified are [list of role categories, e.g., "current donors, program participants, partner orgs, regulators"].

Produce a stakeholder map that:
- Groups stakeholders by influence and interest
- Notes likely concerns for each group
- Identifies any group I have not yet considered
- Suggests an engagement approach for each

Flag any stakeholder group whose voice is structurally underrepresented in this kind of process.
Before you paste: Role categories only, not named individuals.
Pro tip: The "underrepresented voice" question is usually the one to keep working on.

23. Theory of change scaffold

For: a first-pass theory of change before a working session with your team.

I need a scaffold for a theory of change. Our long-term outcome is [specific change in the world]. Our work consists of [primary activities]. The population we serve is [description]. Our key assumption is [the belief underlying the work].

Produce a scaffold with:
- Inputs we bring
- Activities we run
- Outputs (what we produce)
- Outcomes (what changes in the short and medium term)
- Long-term impact

For each layer, flag the assumption that has to be true for the next layer to follow. Name any assumption that seems weak.
Before you paste: Strategic context only. Not the underlying client data or service metrics.
Pro tip: The "weakest assumption" is often the most important question for the team.

24. Risk assessment frame

For: identifying risks for a new program or decision.

I am assessing risks for [new program, expansion, or decision] at [organization type]. Context: [brief].

Produce a risk assessment with the following categories:
- Mission risks (what could undermine the core work)
- Financial risks
- Reputational risks
- Operational risks
- Compliance and legal risks

For each risk, score likelihood (low/medium/high) and impact (low/medium/high), and suggest one mitigation. Flag any risk that is harder to mitigate than to accept.
Before you paste: General categories. No internal compliance issues, donor confidentiality matters, or active legal questions.
Pro tip: Use this as input to a human review, not a substitute for one.

25. Logic model draft

For: a one-page logic model for a program or grant application.

I need a logic model for [program name]. The problem it addresses is [one sentence]. The population served is [description]. Key activities include [2 to 4 activities]. Expected duration: [time frame].

Produce a one-page logic model with:
- Problem statement
- Target population
- Inputs (resources required)
- Activities
- Outputs (units of work)
- Short-term outcomes (within 12 months)
- Long-term outcomes (3 to 5 years)
- One measurable indicator per outcome

Be honest about what is realistic at our scale.
Before you paste: Public program description only. No individual client data.
Pro tip: Pair this with the theory of change scaffold above. They sharpen each other.

Writing help

General-purpose prompts that work across any topic. Reach for these when you have a draft and need to refine it.

26. Reduce length while keeping meaning

For: tightening a draft that is too long.

Here is a draft: [paste text]

Reduce this to [target word count] words. Keep the meaning, the tone, and the structure. Cut filler, redundancy, and anything that does not earn its place. Do not summarize. Tighten.
Before you paste: Drafts about clients or sensitive matters: anonymize first.
Pro tip: Compare line by line. AI sometimes cuts the wrong things.

27. Shift tone

For: making a draft more formal, more conversational, or more direct.

Here is a draft: [paste text]

Rewrite this in a more [formal / conversational / direct / warm] tone. Keep the structure and meaning. Do not add or remove information. Avoid clichés. If a sentence cannot be improved, leave it.
Before you paste: Same as above. Anonymize before pasting drafts about real people.
Pro tip: Ask for two versions of the tone shift. The first attempt often overshoots.

28. Headline brainstorming

For: generating multiple headline or subject line options.

I need a headline for [article type or email type]. The piece is about [brief topic]. Audience: [who will read it]. The action I want them to take is [click, read further, RSVP, etc.].

Generate ten headline options. Vary the approach:
- Two declarative statements
- Two questions
- Two with a concrete number or specific
- Two with mild provocation
- Two with a quieter, observational tone

No clickbait. No emoji.
Before you paste: Public-facing context only.
Pro tip: Test your top three on a colleague who has not seen the underlying article.

29. Plain-language rewrite

For: turning jargon-heavy writing into plain language without losing nuance.

Here is a draft: [paste text]

Rewrite at a [6th / 8th / 10th] grade reading level. Keep the meaning accurate and the tone respectful. Replace jargon with plain words. Use short sentences. Where a technical term must stay, define it in line. Do not flatten nuance. If a concept is genuinely complex, signal that to the reader instead of pretending it is simple.
Before you paste: Anonymize any client-related drafts before pasting.
Pro tip: After the rewrite, ask: "What did you have to oversimplify, and how should I flag that to the reader?"

30. Translate jargon to layperson terms

For: a glossary of plain-language equivalents for a specific document.

Here is a document with industry or technical jargon: [paste text]

Produce a table with three columns:
- The original term as it appears
- A plain-language equivalent
- A one-sentence explanation for someone who has never encountered it

Limit to the 10 to 15 most important terms. Skip jargon that is genuinely common in everyday use.
Before you paste: Source document should be public or de-identified.
Pro tip: Use the output to build a glossary for your team. Share it before the next document with the same jargon goes out.

Adapt these to your organization

These prompts are starting points. The best version is the one that reflects your specific voice, audience, and mission. A few habits that help: save the prompts that work for you in a shared document so staff can reuse and improve them. Add a "what to avoid" line specific to your organization (overused phrases, words your founder hates, sector jargon you have deliberately moved past). When a prompt produces a great result, save the exact wording. When it produces a bad result, save that too. The contrast teaches you what your audience and your tools respond to. Share new prompts with the team rather than hoarding them. The best prompt library is collective.
