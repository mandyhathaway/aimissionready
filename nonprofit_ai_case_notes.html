Case Notes and AI

A careful guide for the workflow that is happening whether or not your organization has approved it
Mandy Hathaway Consulting  ·  mandyhathaway.com  ·  AI Ethics & Technology Consulting

This is one of the most-asked questions in nonprofit AI conversations, and it is the one I am most careful answering. Case managers, clinicians, social workers, and program staff are using AI tools to help them write case notes. They are doing this with good reasons: caseloads are too large, documentation burden is real, and the AI is fast. They are also, in most cases, exposing the most sensitive information their organization holds.

This guide is honest about the situation. The short version: most AI-assisted case note workflows that staff invent on their own are higher risk than the staff realize. The longer version, which is the rest of this document, is about when AI use in case notes is genuinely appropriate, when it is not, and what to offer staff who are clearly drowning and looking for relief.

The question is not whether staff will use AI for case notes. They are already doing it. The question is whether your organization will guide that use or find out about it during an audit.

The default situation, named honestly

Across the human services sector in 2026, the most common AI-on-case-notes workflow looks like this: a staff member finishes a client visit, has rough notes, opens ChatGPT (or Gemini, or Claude, or Copilot), pastes the notes, and asks for a polished progress summary. They edit the output, paste it into the EHR, and move on. The whole thing takes ten minutes instead of thirty.

That workflow, when the AI tool is a free or consumer-tier product, has several layered problems. The client's information has left your organization's control. The data may be retained, used for training, or exposed in a breach. Your HIPAA, FERPA, or state confidentiality posture has been compromised. Your BAA does not cover this. Your client's consent did not extend to it. And the staff member did not know any of this, because nobody told them.

The staff member is not the problem. The information vacuum around what is and is not okay is the problem.

What absolutely should not happen with AI and case notes

Do not do these things

These practices are high-risk for HIPAA violations, state confidentiality breaches, and professional ethics complaints. If they are happening at your organization, address them this week.

  • Pasting case notes that contain client names into any consumer AI tool (ChatGPT free or Plus, Gemini consumer, Claude free, Copilot consumer).
  • Pasting case notes into any AI tool where your organization does not have a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) on file with the AI vendor or with the platform the AI runs on.
  • Using an AI tool to generate progress notes from session recordings that were made on personal devices or stored outside your EHR.
  • Using AI to "fill in the gaps" by inventing detail the staff member did not directly observe. This is fabrication and creates an unreliable record.
  • Using AI to summarize trauma disclosures, suicidal ideation, or other crisis-level content. The risk of factual error in summarization is unacceptable for this category.
  • Pasting information about other people the client mentioned (family members, partners, children, colleagues). Those people did not consent to any of this.
  • Pasting case notes for the purpose of "anonymizing them" by asking the AI to remove names. The information has already left when you paste; removing names afterward does not undo that.

What can be appropriate, with care

None of this is to say AI has no place in clinical or program documentation. It does. The appropriate uses look different from the workflow most staff are inventing on their own.

  • AI features inside your EHR, under a current BAA. If your EHR vendor offers ambient documentation, AI summarization, or dictation features, and the BAA covers those specific features, those uses sit inside your existing compliance posture. (Still verify the BAA actually covers AI features specifically. See the vendor migration guide.)
  • Drafting templates for case notes, with no client information. "Help me write a template for documenting a 30-minute home visit for a housing services client" is a fine prompt. Nothing client-specific enters the AI tool.
  • Rewriting your own training materials or case management policies into plainer language. Internal documents, no client information.
  • Generating questions to ask in your next supervision session about a documentation challenge. Frame the challenge in general terms without identifying details.
  • Improving documentation skills generally. "Show me three examples of strong outcome-focused progress notes for a job placement program" produces useful sample language with no real client involved.
  • On-device or local AI tools that do not transmit data externally. Some EHRs and some local AI tools (e.g., dictation that runs entirely on the device, certain enterprise-deployed local LLMs) can process content without it leaving your infrastructure. Verify before relying on this.

The gray area: AI features baked into the EHR

This is the most rapidly growing category and the one where most nonprofits will encounter AI on case notes. Your EHR vendor adds an "AI summary" or "ambient documentation" feature. The vendor says it is HIPAA-compliant. The feature ships on a default-on basis. Is it okay to use?

Maybe. The questions to answer first:

  1. Does your existing EHR BAA explicitly cover the AI feature? "Our standard BAA covers all features" is not specific enough. Ask the vendor to confirm in writing that the BAA covers this specific feature.

  2. If the AI runs through a third-party provider (Microsoft, OpenAI, Google), is that handoff covered by a chain of BAAs? Some EHR vendors have BAAs with their AI providers. Some have arrangements that are technically compliant but not contractually airtight. Ask for the structure.

  3. Is your client's consent broad enough to cover AI processing of their session content? Standard intake consents written before 2023 usually do not name AI processing. This is worth reviewing with your legal counsel.

  4. Does the AI feature persist data, and for how long? Recordings, transcripts, AI-generated drafts. Each may have a different retention policy.

  5. Can clinicians turn the feature off for specific sessions when the content is unusually sensitive? Crisis sessions, custody-related disclosures, abuse reporting. Per-session control matters.

If you can answer all five with confidence, the EHR-native AI feature may be appropriate. If you cannot, disable it until you can, and notify staff in writing.

What to offer staff who are clearly drowning

The reason staff use AI on case notes is not laziness. It is documentation burden, caseload size, and the gap between what they are asked to produce and the time they have to produce it. Telling them to stop using AI without addressing the underlying pressure is not going to work.

Things that can genuinely reduce documentation burden without exposing client data:

  1. Template-driven documentation. Build well-designed templates inside your EHR that capture the required fields without requiring staff to write long narrative each time. AI can help you build the templates, using no real client data.

  2. Voice-to-text without cloud AI. Many phones and laptops now have on-device speech-to-text that does not transmit audio to a cloud service. Staff can dictate notes to their device, paste the text into the EHR, and edit. This is meaningfully different from cloud-based ambient documentation.

  3. Realistic time allocation. If your documentation expectations require staff to invent shortcuts, the expectations are the problem. AI is not going to fix a structural workload issue. Pretending it can is how organizations end up with HIPAA violations.

  4. EHR-native AI under proper BAA. If your EHR offers this and you have done the verification above, this can be the legitimate version of the workflow staff are inventing. Make it official, train staff on its boundaries, and disable the consumer AI alternative.

  5. Peer review of documentation practices. Sometimes staff write longer notes than necessary because they are uncertain about what to include. Coaching and peer review can reduce documentation time more than any AI tool, with no privacy cost.

The conversation with staff

When you discover staff are using consumer AI tools on case notes, the response is not disciplinary. It is informational. Most staff doing this have no idea about the data handling consequences. The conversation looks something like:

Sample staff conversation

"Thank you for being honest about this. I want to talk about it because the tools that help most with notes also create risks I don't think we have explained well enough. Here is what happens when you paste case notes into [tool name]. Here is why that matters for [client population]. Here is what you can use instead, and here is the timeline for getting you a real solution that actually saves you time. Until that is in place, please stop using [tool name] for anything containing client names or session details. If you are unsure whether something counts, send it to me before you paste."

This conversation, repeated as needed, plus an actual fix for the underlying documentation burden, is the work. Punishment will produce hidden use. Information plus a real alternative will produce changed behavior.

The honest summary

If your organization handles PHI, FERPA-covered records, attorney-client privileged information, or anything else covered by professional ethics or state confidentiality statutes, the default answer for "should staff use consumer AI tools on case notes" is no. Not because AI is bad. Because the data handling of consumer AI tools is not compatible with the confidentiality obligations your clients trusted you with.

The not-default answer, for organizations that have done the verification work, is "yes, under specific conditions, using specific tools, with specific data hygiene, and under specific BAAs." Getting to that answer is the work of this guide, the vendor migration guide, and the governance guide taken together.

The middle answer, which is where most nonprofits actually live in 2026, is "we have not made a decision and staff are making their own." That is the situation this guide is for. If you read this and recognize your organization, the first move is not a policy. It is a conversation with the staff member you suspect is doing it, and a real plan to address the documentation burden that drove them there.
